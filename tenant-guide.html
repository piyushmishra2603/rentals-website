Chapter 02
How to Search for the Right Property
Search smarter, not harder — find properties that genuinely match your needs
🤖 Our AI search analyses your commute distance, budget range, and lifestyle preferences to rank results — not just keyword matches.
01
Define Your Requirements First
Before searching, decide: your maximum budget (rent + deposit + bills), preferred locality (commute under 30 min), essential amenities (WiFi, meals, AC), and move-in date. Vague searches waste time.
02
Search by Locality, Not Just City
Search for specific areas: "Kondapur", "Koramangala 5th Block", "Andheri West" — not just "Hyderabad". Use the map view to see properties relative to your workplace or college.
03
Use Filters Strategically
Filter by: type (PG/flat/hostel), budget range, gender preference, furnishing, amenities (WiFi, meals, AC, gym), and availability date. Verified listings only = fewer scams.
04
Read Listings Carefully
Check: exact address vs nearby landmarks, what's included (utilities, WiFi, meals), house rules (curfew, guests, cooking, smoking), and minimum commitment period before calling.
05
Contact Owner Directly
Our platform shows direct owner contact — no broker numbers. Call to confirm availability, ask about actual bills, and schedule a visit. Always visit before committing.
⚠️
Scam Warning — Red Flags
Be suspicious of listings that: are priced far below market rate, ask for deposit before visiting, request payment via UPI before signing any agreement, have stock photos instead of real images, or owners who are "abroad" and can't meet in person. Never pay deposit without a signed agreement and physical inspection.

Chapter 04
Property Inspection — What to Check
Never commit to a property without visiting in person and checking every corner

A physical visit is non-negotiable. Photos can be misleading — always inspect before paying any money. Here's your complete inspection checklist.

Structural & Infrastructure
✓
Check for water seepage on walls or ceiling — especially in corners and near AC units
✓
Test every electrical switch, socket, and light point before signing
✓
Run all taps and check water pressure — flushing toilet, shower, kitchen
✓
Check drain flow speed — slow drains indicate plumbing issues
✓
Test window and door locks — ensure all operate smoothly and securely
✓
Check ventilation and natural light in every room — especially bedrooms
⚠
Visit at different times (evening and day) to assess noise levels from traffic, neighbours
⚠
Check mobile signal strength in your carrier inside the property
Amenities to Verify in Person
✓
Test the WiFi speed if it's advertised — run a speed test on your phone
✓
Check AC — does it cool properly? Is it maintained or rattling?
✓
Check parking — is your specific slot clearly assigned?
✓
If meals are included — actually eat one meal before committing
✓
Meet current tenants if possible — they'll tell you the truth about the property and landlord
✓
Check lift functionality and availability of stairs if above 3rd floor
📸
Document Everything at Inspection
Take photos and videos of every room, noting all existing damage — scratches, stains, marks, broken items. Share them with the owner via WhatsApp and save screenshots. This documentation protects your deposit when you vacate. Without it, unscrupulous owners can claim pre-existing damage was caused by you.
Chapter 05
Rent Negotiation — Tips & Scripts
Most owners expect negotiation — here's how to do it effectively without being awkward

Rent is almost always negotiable, especially for longer commitments or immediate move-in. A property vacant for 30+ days costs the owner ₹8,000–₹50,000 — they are motivated to compromise.

How to Research Before Negotiating
💡
Check 5–8 similar properties in the same locality on SmartRent AI — note the average rent
💡
Check how long the listing has been live — older listings = more negotiating leverage
💡
Note any negatives (top floor no lift, road-facing noise, older building) as leverage points
💡
Find out the previous tenant's rent if possible — the baseline for your negotiation
Effective Negotiation Strategies
01
Lead with a Long Commitment
"I'm planning to stay for 2 years minimum" — owners prize stable, long-term tenants. This alone can get you 5–10% discount.
02
Offer Advance Rent
Offering 2–3 months' rent in advance is a strong signal of reliability and can justify a lower monthly rate.
03
Negotiate the Deposit More Than Rent
If the owner won't budge on rent, negotiate a lower deposit. Reducing deposit from 5 months to 3 months frees up ₹20,000+ in working capital for you.
04
Ask for Amenity Inclusions
If the rent won't drop, ask for add-ons: "Can you include WiFi?" or "Can the painting be done before move-in?" — these have monetary value.
05
Use a Polite Anchor
If the rent is ₹15,000 and market rate is ₹13,000, say: "I've seen similar properties at ₹12,500–13,000 in this area. Could we agree on ₹13,500?" Always anchor 10–15% below your actual target.
💬
Script: How to Start the Rent Negotiation
"I really like the property and I'm ready to move in immediately. I've done my research and similar flats in this area are going for ₹[X]. Would you consider ₹[Y]? I'm planning a long stay and will be a reliable tenant." — Simple, honest, and effective.

Understanding Your Rental Agreement
Every clause you need to read before you sign — simplified

Never sign any document you haven't fully read and understood. A rental agreement is a legal contract. Below are the critical clauses and what to watch out for.

⚠️
Critical Warning — Biometric Clause
Do NOT sign an agreement that contains clauses like: "Tenant waives right to legal recourse", "Owner may evict without notice", or "Tenant is responsible for all structural repairs". These are legally unenforceable but can cause harassment. Ask for such clauses to be removed before signing.
✓

✓

✓
Keep a physical copy and a scanned digital copy in Google Drive or email
⚠
Do not accept verbal agreements — courts generally do not recognise them

Chapter 08
Your Legal Rights as a Tenant
Know your rights — you have more protection than most tenants realise

Indian tenancy law protects tenants significantly. Most tenants are unaware of these rights and are exploited. Understanding them costs you nothing and could save you lakhs.

Fundamental Tenant Rights
⚖️
Right to Quiet Enjoyment: The owner cannot enter your premises without 24–48 hours' prior notice. Unannounced entry is a trespass violation
⚖️
Right to Habitable Conditions: The owner must maintain basic habitability — functional water, electricity, and sanitation. You can withhold rent if the owner fails to make essential repairs (after proper notice)
⚖️
Right Against Discriminatory Eviction: Owners cannot evict you based on religion, caste, gender, diet, state of origin, or marital status
⚖️
Right to Deposit Refund: Your deposit must be returned within 30 days of vacating (14 days in some states) with an itemised deduction statement
⚖️
Right to Receipt: You are entitled to a written receipt for every rent and deposit payment
⚖️
Protection Against Illegal Eviction: The owner CANNOT lock you out, cut utilities, or remove your belongings without a court order — doing so is a criminal offence
What to Do If Your Rights Are Violated
01
Send a Written Legal Notice
Document the violation and send a registered post letter to the owner specifying the violation and demanding remedy within 15 days. This creates a legal paper trail.
02
Approach Rent Authority (Model Tenancy Act States)
States that have adopted the Model Tenancy Act (AP, TN, Uttarakhand, UP) have dedicated Rent Authorities. File a complaint online — faster and cheaper than civil court.
03
File in Consumer Court
For issues like illegal deposit withholding, consumer courts offer fast remedy. Claims under ₹50 lakh are handled by District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission — typically resolved within 90 days.
04
Police Complaint for Illegal Eviction
If an owner locks you out, cuts utilities, or threatens you — file an FIR immediately. This is a criminal offence (Section 441 IPC for criminal trespass, Section 427 for damage to property).
⚖️
Model Tenancy Act 2021 — Your New Protections
Caps security deposit: 2 months for residential, 6 months for commercial. Mandates written agreements. Creates Rent Authority for fast dispute resolution. Prevents arbitrary evictions. Check if your state has adopted it — AP, TN, UT, UP, Assam have enacted it.
Chapter 09
Protecting & Recovering Your Security Deposit
The most contested issue in Indian rentals — here's how to always get your money back

Security deposit disputes are the #1 tenant complaint in India. The key is prevention through documentation — not fighting after the fact.

How to Protect Your Deposit from Day 1
✓
Photo/video document every existing defect at move-in and share with owner in writing
✓
Keep all maintenance request messages (WhatsApp screenshots are valid evidence)
✓
Keep receipts for any repairs you paid for that the owner should have covered
✓
Conduct a pre-vacating walkthrough with the owner at least 1 week before leaving
✓
Hand over the property in the same condition as you received it — get written acknowledgment
⚠
Professional cleaning (₹1,500–₹5,000) before handover almost always prevents cleaning disputes
Legitimate vs Illegal Deductions
Deduction TypeLegitimate?Notes
Unpaid rent or utility bills✅ YesMust show bills and proof of non-payment
Broken fixtures (by tenant)✅ YesMust be documented damage, not pre-existing
Deep cleaning after long vacancy of mess✅ YesProfessional quote must be provided
Normal wear and tear (paint peeling, minor scuffs)❌ NoRoutine wear is owner's responsibility
Painting (routine 3-year cycle)❌ NoUnless tenant caused unusual damage
"Miscellaneous charges" without itemisation❌ NoAll deductions must be specific and documented
Damage that existed at move-in (pre-documented)❌ NoYour move-in photos are your proof
Society charges (owner's responsibility)❌ NoUnless specifically agreed in the agreement
If Your Deposit Is Withheld Unfairly
01
Send a Formal Demand Notice
Email + registered post. State the deposit amount, payment date, vacancy date, and demand return within 15 days. Mention that you will approach consumer court/legal authority if not returned.
02
File in Consumer Court
File a complaint at your District Consumer Forum. Cost: ₹200–₹1,000. No lawyer required for amounts under ₹50 lakh. You can get the deposit + 18% interest + compensation for harassment.
03
Small Claims / Lok Adalat
Lok Adalat (Legal Services Authority) offers free mediation. Perfect for deposit disputes under ₹1 lakh. Both parties attend one session, settlement is legally binding and usually achieved within days.
✅
Legal Reality Check
Indian courts consistently rule in favour of tenants for deposit disputes where the tenant has: (1) A signed agreement specifying deposit, (2) Bank records of deposit payment, (3) Move-in condition documentation, (4) Evidence of clean handover. Build this evidence from Day 1.
Chapter 10
Frequently Asked Tenant Questions
Real answers to questions every tenant has but is afraid to ask
Can my landlord increase rent without notice? ▼
No. Rent can only be increased as per the escalation clause in your signed agreement. If your agreement says 5% annual increase after 11 months, that is the only valid increase. Any increase beyond the agreed clause requires your written consent. Oral announcements of rent hikes are not legally binding.
My landlord is asking for cash rent — is that legal? ▼
Under Section 269ST of the Income Tax Act, no person can receive cash of ₹2 lakh or more in a single transaction. For monthly rent this usually doesn't apply, but it's always better to pay via bank transfer or UPI for your own protection. Cash payments without receipts leave you with no proof of payment if the landlord denies receiving rent later.
Can I sub-let my rented flat to another person? ▼
Generally no, unless your agreement explicitly permits subletting with owner's consent. Sub-letting without permission is a breach of agreement and can be grounds for eviction. However, you can negotiate subletting permission into your agreement if you foresee needing it.
I've paid the deposit but the owner isn't handing over keys — what do I do? ▼
This is fraud if the deposit was paid and possession is being withheld without reason. Send a written demand via WhatsApp and email immediately. If no response within 24–48 hours, file a police complaint for cheating (Section 415/420 IPC). Always pay deposits only after the agreement is signed and you've confirmed the date for key handover.
My landlord suddenly wants me to vacate — do I have to leave? ▼
You do not have to leave immediately. The owner must give written notice as per the notice period in your agreement (usually 30–60 days). Even then, if you believe the eviction is unjust (e.g., the owner wants to sell and you have remaining tenancy), you can approach the Rent Authority or file a civil suit for injunction. Only a court order can compel you to vacate.
What happens to my deposit if the owner sells the property? ▼
Your deposit follows the tenancy, not the person. The new owner legally inherits the obligation to return your deposit. Your lease also survives the sale — the new owner must honour your remaining lease terms. Get this clause explicitly in your agreement: "The deposit obligation transfers to any new owner/transferee of the property."
The PG has house rules that feel restrictive — are they enforceable? ▼
Rules that are part of the signed agreement are contractually binding. However, rules that violate constitutional rights (based on religion, caste, diet, gender) or criminal law cannot be enforced. Rules like "no vegetarian cooking" (violates Article 21) or "no persons of specific religion" are void and illegal. Reasonable rules like visitor timings, common area cleanliness, and quiet hours are valid and enforceable.
Do I need to pay TDS from my rent payments? ▼
If you are an individual or HUF paying rent exceeding ₹50,000 per month to an individual/HUF landlord, you must deduct TDS at 5% under Section 194IB and deposit it using Form 26QC. This applies only to individuals paying high rent. For most standard rentals under ₹50,000/month, no TDS obligation arises for the tenant.
